Ithaca: Cornell University press, 1988.
Reviewed by Bruce La Brack(University of the Pacific)
Excerpted from the Journal of American Ethnic History, Summer 1991, pp. 141-142

"While based on a relatively small sample of forty-two families, the length of the study and the wealth of detail make this book a superb introduction to Punjabi Sikh immigrant life to California. It also makes clear the mutual impacts that result from the presence of Asian Americans within the larger community and of their children in a multicultural, multilingual, and multiracial school context. Moreover, the author's attention to general patterns and educational issues beyond a narrow ethnic focus will make the book useful for a variety of researchers from educational administrators and scholars of comparative/international education to educational anthropologists and politicians involved with academic and policy issues related to immigration and education."

"Although well beyond the stated scope of the book, there are enough references and statistics on differences between the Sikh and Mexican-American student populations to make the reader interested in learning more, particularly as what is presented indicates how potentially profitable and revealing a more systematic, comparative analysis between these two groups might be."

"This study reveals the unfortunate but not surprising fact that the school system itself presents many barriers, often unconsciously, to minority achievement. Its major contribution is to show how Sikhs, although perceived as radically exotic by many of the surrounding majority, share many important values and attitudes that parallel so-called American ideals, specifically the value of hard work, commitment to family, independence, religious devotion, and economic and academic success. That Sikh children are succeeding at a time when many other Americans are asking what is wrong with the educational system and reexamining assumptions about curriculum and pedagogy makes this book a major contribution to the current debates which, eventually, are likely to fundamentally alter the philosophy of American education."

Gibson, Margaret A.
Accommodation Without Assimilation: Sikh Immigrants in an American High School.
Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 1988.
Reviewed by David M. Fetterman (Stanford University)
Excerpted from American Anthropologist, vol. 92, 1990, p. 218.

"This book is a real contribution to the field of educational anthropology. Gibson describes Punjabi Sikh immigrants in an American high school, based on her two-year study....she demonstrates how Punjabi immigrant students often outperform both majority and long-established minority students, even though there is significant cultural conflict between home and school life, little direct parental involvement, prejudice, language proficiency problems, and a depressed socioeconomic status. Gibson explains how Punjabi students are willing to subordinate specific cultural practices, accommodating the dominant group, while maintaining their cultural identity. Punjabis "encourage their children to adopt the 'good' ways of 'the Americans', according to Gibson (p.24)"
